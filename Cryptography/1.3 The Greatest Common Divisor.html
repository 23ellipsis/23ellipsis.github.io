1.3 The Greatest Common Divisor

If a and b are integers, then d is called a common divisor if d|a and d|b (that is, if d divides both a and b).

The Greatest Common Divisor (GCD) is the largest integer that divides both a and b.

Coprimes

The integers a and b are Relatively Prime (or Coprime) if GCD(a,b) = 1. In other words, a is considered prime relative to b (and vice versa) if their greatest common divisor is 1.

If the largest integer that divides both a and b is 1, then it logically follows that it is the only integer that divides them both.

Remember: A prime number is only divisible by itself and 1.

Finding the GCD

There are different ways to find the GCD, and one of them is using Canonical Decomposition.
Read The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic

Suppose the integers a and b can be expressed as:
a = p1k1 · p2k2 · p3k3 ...
b = p1m1 · p2m2 · p3m3 ...

Then the Greatest Common Divisor of a and b is given by:
GCD(a, b) = p1min(k1, m1) · p2min(k2, m2) · p3min(k3, m3) ...

Example: finding the GCD of a = 60 and b = 48.

First, express both numbers as products of prime powers:
60 = 22 · 31 · 51
48 = 24 · 31

Now take each common prime factor and choose the smallest exponent for each:
GCD(60, 48) = 2min(2, 4) · 3min(1, 1) = 22 · 31

Finally, simplify: GCD(60, 48) = 4 · 3 = 12

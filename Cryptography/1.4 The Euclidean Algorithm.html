1.4 The Euclidean Algorithm

Let a and b be two positive integers where a > b, and let r be the remainder when a / b. Then:

GCD(a, b) = GCD(b, r)

Explanation

Assume we have two numbers a and b, with a being larger. The Euclidean Algorithm states that to find GCD(a, b), we can instead find GCD(b, r) where r is the remainder of a / b. This approach is more efficient when a and b are large.

Visualizing the Process

The Euclidean Algorithm works because the greatest common divisor of a and b cannot be larger than the smaller number (b).

For example, GCD(90, 30) = 30, because 30 is the largest integer that divides both numbers, and it's also the largest possible value that could be considered the GCD under the right conditions (which is dividing both 30 and 90).
When the smaller integer does not perfectly divide the larger one, the GCD must be less than the smaller integer. For example, GCD(80, 30) must be less than 30, because any number larger than 30 will not divide 30, so it won't divide both numbers (30 and 80) to be considered a common divisor, let alone the greatest common divisor.

Using the Euclidean Algorithm to find GCD(80, 30) reduces the problem as follows:
GCD(80, 30) → GCD(30, 20)
What happened is that we removed full multiples of 30 from 80 (two 30's exist inside 80), leaving only the remainder (20).

Example

Find GCD(252, 105) using the Euclidean Algorithm:

  1. Divide 252 by 105:
    252 = 105 × 2 + 42 (remainder r = 42).
    Now, GCD(252,105) = GCD(105,42).
  2. Divide 105 by 42:
    105 = 42 × 2 + 21 (remainder r = 21).
    Now, GCD(105,42) = GCD(42,21).
  3. Divide 42 by 21:
    42 = 21 × 2 + 0 (remainder r = 0).
    Since the remainder is 0, the GCD is the last non-zero divisor: GCD = 21.

Therefore, GCD(252,105) = 21.

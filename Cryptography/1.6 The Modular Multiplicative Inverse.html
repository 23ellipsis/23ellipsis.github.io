1.6 The Modular Multiplicative Inverse

A non-zero element a belongs to Zm is called a unit or invertible if there exists an element b in Zm such that (a × b) mod m ≡ 1.

In this case, b is called the multiplicative inverse of a, denoted b = a-1.

Outside of modulo, a is the multiplicative inverse of b if a*b = 1 (the multiplicative inverse of a is always 1/a).

An element a in Zm is a unit if and only if a and m are relatively prime:
GCD(a, m) = 1.

This simply means that in order to check whether integer a has a multiplicative inverse in modulo m, we just have to check whether a and m are relatively prime.

If p is a prime, then GCD(a, p) = 1 for all a ∈ {1, 2, ..., p - 1}. Thus every non-zero element in Zp is a unit.

Note that we only check inverses for numbers from 0 to m - 1 because every other number can be reduced modulo m.

Finding the multiplicative inverse:

The Extended Euclidean Algorithm provides a systematic way to find the multiplicative inverse a-1 of a non-zero element a in Zm.

Example: Find the multiplicative inverse of 3 modulo 11

This means: find x such that 3 × x ≡ 1 (mod 11)

Using the Extended Euclidean Algorithm:

  1. Divide 11 by 3:
    11 = 3 × 3 + 2 (remainder r = 2).
    Now, GCD(11, 3) = GCD(3, 2).
  2. Divide 3 by 2:
    3 = 2 × 1 + 1 (remainder r = 1).
    Now, GCD(3, 2) = GCD(2, 1).
  3. Divide 2 by 1:
    2 = 1 × 2 + 0 (remainder r = 0).
    Since the remainder is 0, the GCD is the last non-zero divisor: GCD = 1.

Now that we've confirmed that a multiplicative inverse exists for 3 in modulo 11, we can proceed to find it using Back Substitution, to end up expressing 1 as a combination of 3 and 11:

  1. 1 = 3 − 2 × 1 (this is from step 2 of the Euclidean Algorithm with the numbers rearranged to solve for 1)
  2. Recall from before that 2 = 11 − 3 × 3, so substitute:
    1 = 3 − (11 − 3 × 3) × 1 = 3 − 11 + 3 × 3.
  3. Simplify:
    1 = 3 × 4 − 11 × 1.
  4. This means that 1 can be written as a linear combination of 3 and 11, which is 1 = 3 × 4 − 11 × 1.
    And thus the multiplicative inverse of 3 modulo 11 is 4.

(3 × 4) mod 11 = 12 mod 11 = 1

Therefore, 3-1 ≡ 4 (mod 11).

Visualizing the process

The linear combination expresses 1 as a sum of multiples of 3 and 11. Since we’re working mod 11, any term that’s a multiple of 11 doesn’t matter (for example, 11 × 100 is still congruent to 0 (mod 11)).

That means the 11 × (1) part vanishes, leaving just 3 × 4. Since 3 × 4 ≡ 1 (mod 11), this shows that 4 is the multiplicative inverse of 3 modulo 11.
